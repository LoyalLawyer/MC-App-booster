What They're Known For Right Now

A&O Shearman is the largest Magic Circle firm by headcount following its transformative 2023 merger with US firm Shearman & Sterling. The merger was the biggest in legal history and created a genuinely transatlantic powerhouse — but integration remains ongoing and is a key story to follow.

Their strongest practices right now are banking & finance, capital markets and M&A. They are particularly dominant in leveraged finance and high-yield debt in Europe. The firm is also a top-three player in financial regulatory work globally.

Post-merger, the combined firm has significant overlap in offices and practices — some rationalisation is expected over 2026. This is something to be aware of and shows you're thinking critically if you mention it in an application.

Strategic Direction 2025–26

  • Doubling down on US private equity and M&A mandate flow post-Shearman merger
  • Building out the Middle East — significant Riyadh and Abu Dhabi growth
  • Targeting the sovereign wealth fund market (ADIA, PIF, QIA) as a strategic client segment
  • Technology and AI practice is expanding significantly — over 100 tech-sector mandates in 2025

Culture & Trainee Life

Trainees describe A&O Shearman as "ambitious and a little intense post-merger" — the culture is more formal than Linklaters or Freshfields, but there's a genuine meritocracy feel. International secondments are a major draw — nearly all trainees do at least one overseas seat.

The trainee intake is one of the largest in the Magic Circle (~120 per year). Some trainees note the firm still feels like two cultures merging — which creates opportunity for those who are adaptable.

What They're Known For Right Now

Clifford Chance is the most globally diversified of the Magic Circle — genuinely present in major markets rather than being London-centric with outposts. Their strongest current practices are capital markets, private equity and dispute resolution.

CC has a strong reputation in financial regulatory work and is a go-to firm for complex cross-border M&A involving multiple jurisdictions. Their New York office is growing rapidly — the 2026 PE partner hire from Freshfields signals ambition in the US PE market.

Strategic Direction 2025–26

What They're Known For Right Now

Freshfields is having its strongest run in a decade. The US expansion strategy — ~500 US lawyers, up from ~200 five years ago — has broken the firm into the global top five M&A advisers by deal value. This is genuinely unprecedented for a London-headquartered firm.

Their M&A, antitrust/competition and disputes practices are elite globally. Freshfields is the firm of choice for hostile takeovers, complex regulatory clearances and bet-the-company litigation.

Strategic Direction 2025–26

  • Continued aggressive US growth — targeting top-10 US PE firms as clients
  • Record 2025 revenues signal the US bet is paying off — more lateral hires planned
  • Antitrust/competition practice is the busiest in its history given global regulatory scrutiny of tech M&A
  • Disputes and international arbitration growing — geopolitical complexity driving demand

What Makes Slaughter and May Different

Slaughter and May is deliberately different from the other four. It has intentionally stayed small, resisted US expansion, has no billing targets, and works through a "best friend" network of independent local firms rather than opening its own offices globally.

The quality of work is exceptionally high — the firm is consistently instructed on the most complex and sensitive UK M&A, corporate governance and financial regulation matters. It advises a disproportionate share of FTSE 100 companies relative to its size.

The culture is genuinely collaborative, less hierarchical and less pressured than peers. Trainees get more early responsibility because the firm is smaller. NQ retention rate is very high.

Strategic Direction 2025–26

  • Signals of private credit expansion — would be a significant strategic shift
  • Deliberately holding firm on its distinctive model while peers chase US market
  • Benefiting from clients who want deep UK expertise rather than global sprawl
  • Pay structure differs: no lock-step, biannual reviews — competitive but not matching £150k peers yet
